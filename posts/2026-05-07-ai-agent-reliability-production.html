AI Agent Reliability in Production: What Actually Works

2026-05-07
AI Agents Reliability Observability Production DevOps

I've spent the last few months running autonomous AI agents in production — trading bots that run 24/7, job search automators, engagement crawlers on Bluesky, background infrastructure tasks. Not pet projects. Real agents making real decisions and taking real actions.

Here's what I've learned: An AI agent is only as reliable as its ability to fail quietly and recover sanely.

It's not about having a smarter model. It's not about prompt engineering perfection. It's about building the scaffolding that lets agents operate safely when you're not watching.

The Three Categories of Failure

When an AI agent fails in production, it falls into one of three buckets:

  1. Loud failure — The agent crashed, threw an exception, and you got alerted. This is fine.
  2. Silent failure — The agent ran, returned no error, and did absolutely nothing useful. This is terrifying.
  3. Wrong failure — The agent decided to do something confidently incorrect, and you didn't notice until harm was done. This is a disaster.

Most platforms focus on preventing loud failures. Smart teams focus on preventing silent failures.

Example: My Trading Bot

I deployed an autonomous trading bot last week. It runs LaunchAgent on my home server, checks market conditions every 5 minutes, and decides whether to enter or exit positions based on technical analysis and portfolio balance.

v1 blew up. Not spectacularly—more like wet fizzle. The bot was running fine (no errors), but it was entering trades with such small edge that transaction fees were eating all gains. It then held losing positions until staleness timeouts expired. Over a week, that became -€250 in drawdown.

The problem? I had no signal that something was wrong. The agent was doing exactly what it was told to do, just on bad assumptions about entry quality and risk sizing. It took me manually reviewing trade logs to catch it.

v2 fixed it differently than you'd expect:

  • Tighter entry criteria — RSI 25-35, confidence >60%, wider spread requirements. This means fewer trades but higher conviction.
  • Extended stale timeout — 4h → 12h. Don't panic-exit on noise.
  • Larger target positions — Better odds that transaction costs don't dominate.
  • Daily state reporting to Discord — Morning and evening snapshots of portfolio health, active positions, unrealized P&L. Not for panic-trading, just for knowing what's happening.
  • 36-hour optimization pass — Cron job that reviews the past trades, calculates Sharpe ratio, win rate, drawdown metrics, and generates a report with recommendations. It doesn't auto-tune (that's a death trap for autonomous systems), but it gives me signal that something needs adjusting.

None of this is model-related. It's all operational infrastructure.

The Job Search Automator: Context Leaks

My job search bot scans LinkedIn, extracts opportunities, tracks application states, and surfaces stale applications (30+ days without movement) and interview-stage updates to my calendar and Google Sheets.

Early on, it would sometimes misidentify job titles, mislabel seniority levels, and occasionally flag positions that weren't actually open. These errors cascaded into wrong spreadsheet entries and duplicate applications.

The fix wasn't a better prompt. It was structured output validation:

  • Every extracted field is schema-validated before it touches the database. Bad data doesn't silently corrupt state.
  • Extracted entities (company names, job titles, application dates) are cross-referenced against canonical sources before storage.
  • Duplicate detection runs before anything is written — compare against existing company/role/date combinations.
  • A weekly audit report shows me what data got rejected, why, and which source patterns were noisy.

Now when the bot encounters ambiguous input, it either flags it for manual review or safely skips it. No silent data corruption.

The Bluesky Engagement Bot: Knowing When to Shut Up

This one taught me the most. I built an autonomous agent that crawls my Bluesky mentions, finds substantive technical conversations, and replies with my takes on infrastructure, DevOps, and AI architecture.

First iteration? Embarrassing. It replied to everything with positive sentiment, completely missing tone and context. It replied to joke threads with serious technical takes. It replied when I'd already replied. It made me look like a bot, because I was one.

The fix wasn't smarter replies. It was smarter filtering:

  • Signal-to-noise classification — Filter out casual banter, jokes, and conversations where I'd already participated. Only flag substantive technical threads.
  • Conversation context window — Look back at the whole thread, not just the mention. Understand who's participating, what's already been said, and whether my voice would actually add value.
  • Tone matching — If the conversation is dry wit and absurdism, match that tone. Don't throw a serious technical essay into a joke thread.
  • High-threshold engagement — Only reply if the thread is active (recent posts), substantive (technical depth), and genuinely missing a perspective I could offer.
  • State tracking — Keep a cache of what I've already replied to this week, across multiple runs. Don't spam the same thread with duplicate takes.

The bot now replies to maybe 3-5 threads per day instead of 15+. Quality went up. I don't look like a spambot anymore.

Lesson: The best AI agent decision is often to do nothing.

Patterns I've Learned

1. Observability First

You can't manage what you can't see. For every autonomous agent, I track:

  • Execution count (how many times did it run?)
  • Decision count (how many times did it decide to act?)
  • Action count (how many times did it actually do something?)
  • Error count (failures)
  • Silent count (ran, decided nothing, took no action)
  • Decision velocity (decisions per hour/day)
  • Action latency (time from decision to completion)

If your agent is running but not deciding, or deciding but not acting, that's signal. If the ratios change unexpectedly, something broke.

2. Graceful Degradation Over Perfection

When an agent is uncertain, it should say so and defer to a human, not confidently hallucinate. Build systems where the agent can:

  • Skip a decision if confidence is below threshold
  • Flag ambiguous cases for manual review
  • Operate in a "dry run" mode that logs intended actions without executing them
  • Implement automatic rollback (if an action goes wrong, undo it and alert)

Better to under-automate and gradually expand scope than to auto-scale too early and cause damage.

3. State as a First-Class Citizen

Autonomous agents make decisions based on state. Corrupt state = corrupt decisions. Every agent should have:

  • Immutable event log — Every decision and action is logged with timestamp, context, and outcome. Never delete this.
  • Snapshot storage — Periodic snapshots of agent state for replay/audit/debugging.
  • State validation on startup — Check for corruption, inconsistency, or staleness before resuming.
  • Audit trail — Who modified the state? When? Why? (Make this automatic for agent actions.)

This costs a bit in storage and latency. It's worth every byte.

4. The Human in the Loop (Smart Async)

You don't need a human in every decision. But you do need humans in some decisions, and you need to surface those intelligently.

Instead of "ask me before every action," build agents that:

  • Act on high-confidence decisions autonomously
  • Flag medium-confidence decisions for asynchronous human review (batch them, don't interrupt)
  • Always defer on low-confidence or novel situations
  • Report outcomes and let humans learn what the agent actually did

My job search bot autonomously applies to roles that match my criteria tightly. It flags ambiguous candidates (non-standard titles, unclear reporting lines, etc.) to my calendar. I review them when I have time, not on interrupt.

5. Cost and Latency Visibility

Every agent action has a cost (API calls, compute time, opportunity cost). Track it:

  • Cost per decision
  • Cost per action
  • Total cost per run/day
  • Cost per unit of value (e.g., cost per successful job application, cost per trade entered)

If your agent is spending €5 per day to make €2 in value, that's visible now instead of a surprise at month-end.

What's Next

I'm running three production agents right now (trading, job search, Bluesky). All three have monitoring, rollback capability, and weekly human audit loops built in. None of them make decisions I couldn't explain or reverse.

The next challenge: scaling these patterns to more agents without exploding operational overhead. That probably means better tooling, more automation of the monitoring itself, and clearer patterns for how to build reliable agents.

But the core insight stands: Reliability isn't about smarter AI. It's about smarter systems around the AI.
